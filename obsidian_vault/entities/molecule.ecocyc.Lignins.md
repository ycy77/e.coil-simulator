## Annotation

Lignins Lignin is a complex phenolic biopolymer and an integral part of the secondary cell walls of plants and some algae. It is one of the most abundant organic polymers on Earth, exceeded only by CELLULOSE, and constitutes from a quarter to a third of the dry mass of wood. During the formation of the secondary cell wall, lignin fills the spaces in the cell wall between the CELLULOSE, Hemicelluloses, and Pectin pectin components, driving out water and strengthening the wall. Lignin is heterogeneous, and lacks a defined primary structure. The illustration below is meant to provide a general concept rather than describe an exact structure. The main basic monomers (known as monolignols) are the phenylpropanoids COUMARYL-ALCOHOL, CONIFERYL-ALCOHOL, and SINAPYL-ALCOHOL (see PWY-361). The composition and relative abundance of the lignols differ among different organisms. The monolignols are glycosylated for the purpose of transport to the lignification zone, but the glucose moiety is removed prior to polymerization (see PWY-83). The biosynthesis of lignin is not well understood. It consists of radical-radical coupling, and is catalyzed by oxidative enzymes (peroxidases and/or laccases). Lignin is covalently linked to the hemicellulose fibers, and thus crosslinks different components of the cell wall, conferring mechanical strength. Lignin can be degraded by certain fungi and bacteria that produce the enzymes MANGANESE-PEROXIDASE-RXN "manganese peroxidase", RXN-17136 "lignin peroxidase" and CELLOBIOSE-DEHYDROGENASE-ACCEPTOR-RXN "cellobiose dehydrogenase".
