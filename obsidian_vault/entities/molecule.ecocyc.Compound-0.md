## Annotation

Compound-0 "Compound 0", Compound-I, and Compound-II are the names given to intermediate states of the heme-iron center that is formed at the active sites of cytochrome P-450 enzymes and peroxidases during catalysis. I. Cytochrome P-450 In cytochrome P-450 the proximal coordinatioin position of the heme iron, which is bound to the 4 nitrogen atoms of the tetrapyrrole ring, is tethered to the protein via a cysteine thiolate ligand. At its rest state, the iron is found at a low-spin ferric state (III valency). At this state the iron also binds a water molecule as a sixth ligand at the distal axial coordination position. Binding of a substrate to the low-spin ferric enzyme leads to displacement of the water as the sixth ligand and a high spin shift. The substrate-bound complex has a greater reduction potential and more easily accepts a single electron from the redox partner, which can be either EC-1.6.2.4 "a cytochrome P450 reductase", Ferredoxins, or Cytochromes-b5). After the first reduction, the heme iron binds oxygen, forming an oxy-heme complex. The oxygen is bound at the distal axial coordination position, from which the water molecule has been displaced. Following the transfer of the second electron from the redox partner and two subsequent protonations, a peroxo adduct is formed, known as Compound-0. Next, the O-O bond is cleaved heterolytically, releasing a water molecule and leaving behind an oxygen atom with only six valence electrons. This highly active oxene oxygen removes one electron from the iron and one from the porphyrin, oxidizing the iron to the unusual valency of IV (ferryl iron) and generating an additional oxidizing equivalent that is delocalized over the porphyrin and thiolate ligands.
