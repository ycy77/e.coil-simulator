## Annotation

The peptide moiety, which is bound through its N terminus to the carboxyl group of muramate and contains alternating L and D amino acids, reveals considerable variation. The amino acid linked directly to muramate is usually L-ALPHA-ALANINE, but in some cases can be replaced by GLY or SER . The most common amino acid in the second position is D-GLT, but in some cases (in a few coryneform bacteria) it is hydroxylated to CPD-48 . In some organisms the α-carboxyl group of D-GLT is modified by amidation or substituted by GLY , by GLYCINAMIDE , or by CPD0-1578 . The greatest variation occurs at position 3. The residue at this position is usually a diamino acid and is involved in cross-linking through the second amino group. The most common residue at this position is MESO-DIAMINOPIMELATE, which is present in probably all Gram-negative bacteria and in many other organisms, such as some species of bacilli, clostridia, lactobacilli, corynebacteria, propionibacteria, Actinomycetales, Myxobacteriales, Rickettsiae, and cyanobacteria (see PEPTIDOGLYCANSYN-PWY). The second common residue at this position is LYS, but other substituition have been documented, including L-ORNITHINE , LL-DIAMINOPIMELATE , CPD-7690 , and 5-HYDROXY-L-LYSINE . In some cases the diamino acid in position 3 is not involved in cross linking. In these cases the residue in position 3 can be N-ACETYL-L-24-DIAMINOBUTANOATE, as in the case of Clavibacter michiganensis , or even a monoamino acid, such as L-ALPHA-ALANINE GLT or HOMO-SER . Position 4 and 5 are almost always occupied by D-ALA-D-ALA di-peptide. In several species enterococci and lactobacilli, the terminal D-ALANINE is replaced by D-LACTATE or D-SERINE , which entails resistance to the glycopeptide antibiotic vancomycin.
