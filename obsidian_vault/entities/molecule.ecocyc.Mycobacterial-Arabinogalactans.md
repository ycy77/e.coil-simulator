## Enriched Summary

Arabinogalactan Arabinogalactans are biopolymers consisting mostly of arabinose and galactose residues. Two main classes of arabinogalactans are found in nature: Plant-Arabinogalactans "plant arabinogalactan" and Mycobacterial-Arabinogalactans "microbial arabinogalactan". The Mycobacterial-Arabinogalactans "microbial arabinogalactan" consists of arabinose and galactose monosaccharides in their furanose configurations and is a major structural component of the mycobacterial cell wall, where it is covalently attached to the peptidoglycan. ## Annotation

Arabinogalactan Arabinogalactans are biopolymers consisting mostly of arabinose and galactose residues. Two main classes of arabinogalactans are found in nature: Plant-Arabinogalactans "plant arabinogalactan" and Mycobacterial-Arabinogalactans "microbial arabinogalactan". The Mycobacterial-Arabinogalactans "microbial arabinogalactan" consists of arabinose and galactose monosaccharides in their furanose configurations and is a major structural component of the mycobacterial cell wall, where it is covalently attached to the peptidoglycan. The galactan portion of the polymer is linear, consisting of approximately 30 units with alternating β-(1-5) and β-(1-6) glycosidic linkages. Arabinans Arabinan chains are attached at three branch points within the galactan chain (believed to be at residues 8, 10 and 12) . These chains consist of about 30 residues . The arabinan portion of the polymer is a complex branched structure with glycosidic linkages of α-(1-3), β-(1-5). The chains are capped by a D-arabinofuranose residue attached by a β-(1-2) linkage. In the Mycobacterial cell wall, the arabinan portion is usually capped with mycolic acids that are attached to the β-(1-2) linked residue. Arabinogalactan molecules in slow-growing mycobacterial strains possess a single galactosamine (GalN) residue attached via an α-linkage to a highly branched arabinofuranose residue in the arabinan core .
