## Enriched Summary

Sialic acids are an important family of related 9-carbon sugars acids, present on the surface of many different cells and functioning in a wide range of different biological processes. N-acetylneuraminate, the most ubiquitous species amongst the sialic acids, is widely distributed in the animal kingdom as a component of oligosaccharides, glycoproteins, and glycolipids. N-acetylneuraminate carries out various biological functions by acting as receptors for microorganisms, viruses, toxins, and hormones, by masking receptors, and by regulation of the immune system. N-acetylneuraminic acid is the predominant form of sialic acid present in humans. It can be found as a terminal sugar on a wide range of surface glycoconjugates . The α-anomer is the form that is found when sialic acid is bound to glycans, but in solution it exists mainly (over 90%) in the β-anomeric form . A number of bacteria that can colonize humans make use of N-acetylneuraminic acid as a nutrient source. In addition, some pathogens such as TAX-727 use sialic acid in an immune evasion mechanism by adding N-acetylneuraminic acid to their lipopolysaccharide , which provides increased survival in human serum.
