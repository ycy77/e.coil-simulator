## Enriched Summary

The Lewis blood group antigens, discovered in 1946, are biosynthetically and structurally related carbohydrate structures used as markers of cell differentiation and embryonic development, based on fucosylation carried by a number of fucosyltransferases. The system is named after Mrs. H. D. G. Lewis, in whose serum the first member of the family was discovered . However, the presence of the Lewis antigens is not limited to erytrocytes, and they are found in different tissues and organs. One of these fucosyltransferases, encoded by the HS10249 gene, is bifunctional and can fucosylate both the 3 and 4 positions of the penultimate sugar in the precursor disaccharide. The gene, also known as the Lewis gene, has a dominant allele (which was named Le) and a recessive non-functional allele (known as le). ## Annotation

The Lewis blood group antigens, discovered in 1946, are biosynthetically and structurally related carbohydrate structures used as markers of cell differentiation and embryonic development, based on fucosylation carried by a number of fucosyltransferases. The system is named after Mrs. H. D. G. Lewis, in whose serum the first member of the family was discovered . However, the presence of the Lewis antigens is not limited to erytrocytes, and they are found in different tissues and organs. One of these fucosyltransferases, encoded by the HS10249 gene, is bifunctional and can fucosylate both the 3 and 4 positions of the penultimate sugar in the precursor disaccharide. The gene, also known as the Lewis gene, has a dominant allele (which was named Le) and a recessive non-functional allele (known as le). The α(1,4)-fucosyltransferase activity is only relevant when the substrates are derived from the beta-Gal-13-beta-GlcNac-R "type 1 precursor disaccharide", as that position is already occupied in the B-Gal-14-NacGlc-R "type 2 disaccharide". The first Lewis antigens to be discovered were the Lewis-a-epitope "Lewis a" and Antigen-Leb "Lewis b" antigens, which are produced from beta-Gal-13-beta-GlcNac-R "type 1 precursor disaccharides" (see PWY-7832). However, the α(1,3) fucosylation of the N-acetylglucosaminyl residue of B-Gal-14-NacGlc-R leads to formation of several related antigens known as the CPD-8594 "Lewis x epitope" (also known as SSEA-1, for stage-specific embryonic antigen 1), Antigen-Ley "Lewis y epitope", and the sLea-Antigen "sialyl-Lewis x epitope" (see PWY-7831).
