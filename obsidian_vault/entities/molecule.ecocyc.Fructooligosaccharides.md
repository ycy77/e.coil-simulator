## Enriched Summary

Fructooligosaccharides (FOS), sometimes called oligofructose or oligofructan, are a class of oligosaccharides used as an artificial or alternative sweetener. A common source of FOS is the hydrolysis of Inulin inulin by EC-3.2.1.7, which results in two types of FOS: either oligosaccharides consisting solely of fructose [(β-D-Fruf-(2→1)-β-D-Fruf)]n (e.g. INULOBIOSE, CPD-24815, and CPD-24816), or oligosaccharides that terminate with glucose [(β-D-Fruf-(2→1)-β-D-Fruf)]n-(2↔1)-α-D-Glcp (e.g. 1-KESTOTRIOSE, NYSTOSE and CPD-24817). The second mechanism that produces FOS is the transfructosylation action of EC-3.2.1.26, on SUCROSE, which results in FOS of the general formula G(Fn) that can contain linkages other than β(1→2) . FOS exhibit sweetness levels between 30 and 50 percent of sugar, and are used as alternative sweeteners. They resist hydrolysis by salivary and intestinal digestive enzymes, but are fermented in the colon by anaerobic bacteria. They are considered probiotics and may stimulate the growth of beneficial bacteria such as Bifidobacteria species . A common source of FOS is the hydrolysis of Inulin inulin by EC-3.2.1.7, which results in two types of FOS: either oligosaccharides consisting solely of fructose [(β-D-Fruf-(2→1)-β-D-Fruf)]n (e.g. INULOBIOSE, CPD-24815, and CPD-24816), or oligosaccharides that terminate with glucose [(β-D-Fruf-(2→1)-β-D-Fruf)]n-(2↔1)-α-D-Glcp (e.g. 1-KESTOTRIOSE, NYSTOSE and CPD-24817). The second mechanism that produces FOS is the transfructosylation action of EC-3.2.1.26, on SUCROSE, which results in FOS of the general formula G(Fn) that can contain linkages other than β(1→2) . FOS exhibit sweetness levels between 30 and 50 percent of sugar, and are used as alternative sweeteners. They resist hydrolysis by salivary and intestinal digestive enzymes, but are fermented in the colon by anaerobic bacteria. They are considered probiotics and may stimulate the growth of beneficial bacteria such as Bifidobacteria species .
