## Annotation

E. coli K-12 is able to swim and swarm by means of flagella - long, semi-rigid protein filaments extending from the cell surface which are rotated at their base in the cell envelope by a molecular motor energized by the proton gradient . Our understanding of flagella-mediated motility in E. coli, draws heavily from studies in Salmonella enterica Typhimurium; flagella structure, assembly and motor function are considered to be widely the same between the two organisms. The flagellum is typically described through a number of substructures including the FLAGELLAR-MOTOR-COMPLEX "basal body" (a multi-subunit transenvelope complex that functions as a reversible rotary motor), the extracellular filament (a polymer of FliC proteins, capped at the distal end by FliD) and the hook (a 'universal joint' connecting the filament to the basal body and comprising a polymer of FlgE plus associated junction proteins FlgK and FlgL). The flagellum also contains a multi-component CPLX0-7451 "export apparatus" which is responsible for the ordered export of proteins during flagella biosynthesis; the export apparatus is located at the base of the basal body and consists of a transmembrane gate complex (FlhA, FlhB, FliP, FliQ, FliR) plus a cytoplasmic ATPase complex (FliH, FliI, FliJ). E. coli is a petrichous bacteria - its several flagella are distributed over the cell surface.
