## Enriched Summary

Cullins Cullins are a family of proteins that provide a scaffold for some RING-type E3 ubiquitin transferases (EC-2.3.2.27 "EC 2.3.2.27"), which are not able to bind a substrate protein directly. These E3 proteins form a complex with a cullin protein and a substrate recognition subunit (SRS). Some complexes also include an adaptor that links the SRS to the complex. The complexes are known as CRLs for Cullin-RING ubiquitin Ligases. CRLs comprise the largest known category of ubiquitin ligases, and regulate an extensive number of dynamic cellular processes, including multiple aspects of the cell cycle, transcription, signal transduction, and development . There are five major types of cullins in metazoa, each of which forms a distinct class of CRL complex, with distinct adaptors and/or substrate-recognition subunits. CRLs are activated by the covalent attachment of the ubiquitin-like protein NEDD8 NEDD8 to the cullin, and are inhibited by binding to the CAND1 inhibitor . The human genome contains eight cullin genes.
