## Enriched Summary

Kanamycins Kanamycins are a group of Aminoglycoside-Antibiotics "aminoglycoside antibiotics" produced by the bacterium TAX-1967. The compounds were discovered in 1957 by researchers in the National Institute of Health of Tokyo, Japan . Due to their wide range of activity, especially against severe staphylococcal and mycobacterial infections, these antibiotics gained almost immediate acceptance, and the most common form, CPD-4821, was produced commercially within a year of its discovery . Kanamycins interact with the 30S subunit of the prokaryotic ribosome, inducing substantial amounts of mistranslation and indirectly inhibiting translocation during protein synthesis, resulting in death . Kanamycins are currently used to treat a wide variety of infections. Kanamycins share several biosynthetic intermediates with other aminoglycosides, including the core moiety CPD-10151 and the pseudodisaccharides CPD-14126, CPD-14125 and CPD-14137. While the main product produced by TAX-1967 is CPD-4821 (R1 = NH2; R2 = OH), additional products are also produced, including CPD-4822 (R1 = R2 = NH2), CPD-4823 (R1 = OH; R2 = NH2) , CPD-14138 (similar to CPD-4821, but contains a glucose moiety instead of the kinosamine moiety) and CPD-4824 (R1 = R2 = OH) .
