## Annotation

CPD0-1099 "Colanic acid" is an exopolysaccharide secreted by TAX-562 and a number of other TAX-543 that encapsulates the organism, resulting in a mucoid appearance . Colanic acid is thought to promote biofilm formation and to protect the organism when exposed to harsh environments such as acidic conditions . Even though it wasn't identified as such, colanic acid was first described in 1927 by Dorothea Smith, who isolated a polysaccharide from a mucoid strain of TAX-562 . Fourteen years later, in a study on the antigens of the Enterobacteriaceae, it was noticed that one antigen was common to several mucoid strains of TAX-57045 . It was soon reported that the same antigen, which was named "M" antigen, is produced by mucoid Escherichia coli strains . In 1954 Kauffmann reported that the antigen was common to all mucoid Salmonella species . Similar exopolysaccharides were reported from a number of mucoid strains and species . In the early 60s it was reported that a large number of Salmonella and Escherichia strains that are not normally mucoid can be induced to form slime exopolysaccharides when grown at 15 to 20°C in high phosphate concentration , and the exopolysaccharide was named "colanic acid" after TAX-562 from which it was isolated . In a paper describing the discovery of the same polysaccharide in TAX-83333 it was suggested that colanic acid and the "M antigen" reported in the earlier studies are identical . A confirmation was obtained in 1969 when it was shown that colanic acid of identical composition is produced by strains from the genera Salmonella, Escherichia, and Aerobacter. At that time the exact structure was not known, though it was known that the polysaccharide contains glucose, galactose, fucose, and glucuronate, as well as some O-acetyl groups . Addition
