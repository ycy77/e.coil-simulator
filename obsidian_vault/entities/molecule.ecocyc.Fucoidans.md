## Annotation

Fucoidan is a generic term used to describe sulfated polysaccharides (MW: average 20,000) found in various species of brown algae such as kombu (edible kelp from mostly the family TAX-33636), limu moui (TAX-86906), bladder wrack (TAX-49266), wakame (TAX-74381), mozuku (TAX-309737), and hijiki (TAX-590727). Fucoidan was first reported in 1913 under the name fucoidin , but the name was changed in 1959 to conform with polysaccharide nomenclature . Fucoidan structure can be divided into two groups depending on their sources: one group includes TAX-33637 species that have their central chains composed by (1→3)-linked α-L-fucopyranose residues ; a second group includes fucoidan isolated from TAX-52968 and TAX-3011 species that have their central chains composed of repeating (1→3) and (1→4) linked α-L-fucopyranose residues . Besides fucose and sulfate, the presence of monosaccharide residues such as mannose, galactose, glucose, xylose and uronic acids were also identified . Several fucose-containing polymers with less than 90% fucosyl residues have also been reported from brown algae and some of them were given names: ascophyllan or xylofucoglycuronan, isolated from TAX-52969, is based on a backbone of mannuronate with branches of 3-O-D-xylosyl-L-fucose-4-sulfate ; sargassan or glycuronofucoglycan, also isolated from TAX-52969, is based on linear chains of D-galactose with branches of L-fucose-3-sulfate or occasionally uronate . Other, generic names in use are F-fucoidan, which consists of mostly fucose , U-fucoidan, which contains approximately 20% of glucuronic acid, and G-fucoidan, which contains a substantial amount of galactose .
