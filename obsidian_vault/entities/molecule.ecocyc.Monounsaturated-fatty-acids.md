## Enriched Summary

Even though fatty acids derived from natural fats and oils, such as CPD-195, are usually at least 8 carbon atoms long, carboxylic acids as short as BUTYRIC_ACID (4 carbon atoms) are considered to be fatty acids. When a fatty acid contains only single bonds, it is referred to as Saturated-Fatty-Acids. When it contains one or more double bonds, it is referred to as Unsaturated-Fatty-Acids. If it contains a double bond in the trans configuration, it is often referred to as a trans fatty acid. Most fatty acids in the trans configuration are not found in nature and are the result of human processing. ## Annotation

Fatty-Acids "A fatty acid" is a carboxylic acid with a long unbranched aliphatic tail. Even though fatty acids derived from natural fats and oils, such as CPD-195, are usually at least 8 carbon atoms long, carboxylic acids as short as BUTYRIC_ACID (4 carbon atoms) are considered to be fatty acids. When a fatty acid contains only single bonds, it is referred to as Saturated-Fatty-Acids. When it contains one or more double bonds, it is referred to as Unsaturated-Fatty-Acids. If it contains a double bond in the trans configuration, it is often referred to as a trans fatty acid. Most fatty acids in the trans configuration are not found in nature and are the result of human processing. About nomenclature: Several nomenclatures are used for unsaturated fatty acids, and are not complementary. Systematic names (or IUPAC names) begin counting the carbon atoms from the carboxylic acid end, and the double bonds are labelled with cis/trans or E/Z notation, e.g. 8E,10E,12Z-octadecatrienoate. However, unsaturated fatty acids are often described using the omega nomenclature, which begins counting the carbon atoms from the terminal methyl carbon (designated as n or ω). In addition, unsaturated fatty acids are often referred to by trivial names, or by the "lipid number" as in 18:3, where 18 is the number of carbons, and 3 is the number of double bonds. This notation is ambiguous, as it does not specify the location of the double bonds.
