## Enriched Summary

Mitogen-Activated-Protein-Kinase "Mitogen-activated protein kinases" (MAP kinase or MAPK) are eukaryotic protein kinases that are specific to the amino acids serine, threonine, and tyrosine. They are found in all eukaryotes including unicellular eukaryotes, fungi, plants, and animals. MAP kinases are involved in directing cellular responses to a diverse array of stimuli, such as mitogens, osmotic stress, heat shock and proinflammatory cytokines. They regulate cell functions including proliferation, gene expression, differentiation, mitosis, cell survival, and apoptosis . MAP kinases are catalytically inactive in their base form. In order to be active, they need to be phosphorylated. Most of the MAP kinases belong to the "classical MAP kinases" family and need to be phosphorylated on two amino acids, a threonine and a tyrosine, which are found in a TxY motif at the activation loop. Phosphorylation of the tyrosine precedes phosphorylation of the threonine. Some MAP kinases are atypical and require only a single residue in their activation loops to be phosphorylated.
