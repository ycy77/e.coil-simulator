## Enriched Summary

Ibuprofen is nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used for pain relief, fever reduction, and against swelling. Like other NSAID drugs (e.g. CPD-524 and CPD-7669), ibuprofen works by inhibiting the enzyme EC-1.14.99.1 (also known as cyclooxygenase or COX) that converts ARACHIDONIC_ACID to PROSTAGLANDIN-H2, a precursor to other prostaglandins that mediate pain, inflammation, and fever. In addition, PROSTAGLANDIN-H2 is also a precursor of ALPHA11-ALPHA-EPOXY-15-HYDROXYTHROMBA, which stimulates platelet aggregation. The inhibition of the COX1 variant of the COX cyclooxygenase also reduces the formation of blood clots. Ibuprofen inhibits both isoforms of cyclooxygenase, HS01815 (COX1) and HS01115 (COX2). Ibuprofen was derived from PROPIONATE during the 1960s. Originally marketed as Brufen, ibuprofen is available under a variety of popular trademarks, including Motrin, Nurofen, Advil, Nuprin and many others. The active form of ibuprofen is CPD-15516. The drug is synthesized as a racemic mixture, but an isomerase encoded by the HS01416 gene converts the inactive CPD-15517 "(R)-form" in vivo to the active form via a coenzyme A derivative .
