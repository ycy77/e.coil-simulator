## Annotation

DcuR regulates several genes involved in C4-dicarboxylate metabolism . In addition, a recent microarray-based global transcriptional profiling study suggested that the DcuR regulon could comprise at least 39 genes, including some involved in lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis and some involved in anaerobic nitrate and nitrite respiration . DcuR is the response regulator in the two-component regulatory system DcuR/DcuS and is a member of the CitAB subfamily of His protein kinases . Autophosphorylation of DcuS is stimulated by C4-dicarboxylates, such as fumarate or succinate, and the phosphate group is transferred to DcuR, which is converted to a dimeric form and becomes more stable . DcuS can also dephosphorylate DcuR . At the membrane, phosphorylated or unphosphorylated DcuR forms a hetero-tetramer complex with phosphorylated or unphosphorylated DcuS, with a low affinity that facilitates DcuR exchange. DcuR is less mobile in its unphosphorylated conformation and is located either in the poles, bound to DcuS, or in the cytosol, bound to promoters . The amount of DcuR increases in the presence of fumarate or upon anaerobic growth, and this increase is an order of magnitude higher than that for DcuS . This protein contains an N-terminal receiver domain that includes the aspartate residue (Asp-56) for phosphorylation and a C-terminal domain that contains a helix-turn-helix motif for DNA binding . Two directly repeated hexameric DNA sequences that are highly enriched in A/T residues have been proposed to be the sites recognized by a DcuR dimer .
