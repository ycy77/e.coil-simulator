## Enriched Summary

Siderophores are small, high-affinity iron chelating compounds usually produced and secreted by microorganisms. Once outside the cell, siderophores bind FE+3 with high affinity, and the iron-siderophore complexes are imported back into the cell by active-transport mechanisms. Siderophores are very common in pathogenic bacteria, since iron in the host organism is usually tightly bound to proteins, resulting in an extremely low concentration of free iron in the blood. Most siderophores belong to one of three major groups: the Catecholate-siderophores "catechols", Hydroxamate-siderophores "hydroxamates" and Thiazoline-siderophores "thiazolines". Some grasses are known to make siderophores as well. These Phytosiderophores "phytosiderophores" are also known as mugineic acids. Hydroxamate siderophores are named based on the presence of a Hydroxamates hydroxamate element in their structures.
