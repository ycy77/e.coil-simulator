## Enriched Summary

LexA represses the transcription of several genes involved in the cellular response to DNA damage or inhibition of DNA replication as well as its own synthesis . This regulation is known as the SOS response . The role of LexA in response to DNA damage appears to be related to the PriA/PriB pathway, which is necessary for DNA replication restart . When DNA is damaged, the RecA coprotease binds to the single-stranded DNA in the damaged region to form a filament . ## Annotation

LexA represses the transcription of several genes involved in the cellular response to DNA damage or inhibition of DNA replication as well as its own synthesis . This regulation is known as the SOS response . The role of LexA in response to DNA damage appears to be related to the PriA/PriB pathway, which is necessary for DNA replication restart . When DNA is damaged, the RecA coprotease binds to the single-stranded DNA in the damaged region to form a filament . This filament interacts with the LexA dimer to activate its self-cleavage activity by an allosteric mechanism, causing the dissociation of LexA from its DNA targets and the induction of the SOS regulon for the repair of broken DNA . The cryo-electron microscopy structure of LexA in complex with RecA filaments revealed that the LexA dimer binds deeply in the groove of RecA filaments . Moreover, key residues in LexA that interact with RecA have been identified . A structurally dynamic peptide loop encoding residues 75-84 of LexA appears to be necessary for the recognition of RecA . LexA interacts with three RecA protomers, with the central subunit providing the most critical contacts, while the L2 loops of adjacent subunits act as 'reinforcers' . The F203 residue in the L2 loop of RecA is a key determinant for its interaction with LexA . This residue inserts into a hydrophobic binding pocket in LexA, triggering its proteolysis . The N-terminal domain (NTD) of LexA plays a crucial role in distinguishing between DNA-bound LexA and free LexA in the cytosol, the latter being in a conformation susceptible to cleavage .
