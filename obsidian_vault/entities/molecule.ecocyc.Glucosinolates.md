## Enriched Summary

The glucosinolates are substituted β-thioglucoside N-hydroxysulfates that are formed from any one of eight amino acids, namely, L-ALPHA-ALANINE, VAL, LEU, ILE, PHE, MET, TYR and TRP. ## Annotation

Glucosinolates "Glucosinolates" are secondary metabolites found in 16 plant families, including agriculturally important crop plants of the TAX-3700 such cabbage, mustard, oilseed rape, and broccoli, as well as the model plant TAX-3702, and are responsible for the typical sharp taste and odor of these plants. The glucosinolates are substituted β-thioglucoside N-hydroxysulfates that are formed from any one of eight amino acids, namely, L-ALPHA-ALANINE, VAL, LEU, ILE, PHE, MET, TYR and TRP. Over 115 naturally occurring glucosinolates have been identified. Much of the diversity amongst glucosinolates arises from the addition of different sized alkyl groups to the side chain of the amino acids, principally valine, phenylalanine and methionine . They are grouped into aliphatic, aromatic, and indolic types, depending on whether they are derived from methionine, phenylalanine and tyrosine, or tryptophan. Indolylmethyl glucosinolates are found mainly in leaves. For a review, see . Glucosinolates are stored in the vacuole and come into contact with the enzyme EC-3.2.1.147 (myrosinase) upon breakage of the cell. Myrosinase de-glucosylates the glucosinolates, resulting in formation of nitriles, isothiocyanates and thiocyanates. The precise product that is formed depends on the glucosinolate and whether an epithiospecifier protein is present. The breakdown products give the typical sharp taste and odor to many of the members of brassicaceae that are eaten, such as raddish and mustard. Arabidopsis has been shown to accumulate at least 23 different glucosinolates in both seeds and leaves.
