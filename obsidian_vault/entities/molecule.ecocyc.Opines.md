## Annotation

Opines Opines are a large family of low molecular weight compounds. The vast majority of opines are secondary amine derivatives resulting from the reduction of the imine formed by condensation of an amino acid with either a keto acid or a sugar. Opines from the CPD-308 family are formed when 2-KETOGLUTARATE serves as the keto substrate in the condensation reaction, while those of the CPD-309 family are formed when PYRUVATE is involved in the condensation reaction. Opines of the CPD-15773 and CPD-26410 families are formed by the condensation of an amino acid with a sugar. A small, separate class of opines is the Agrocinopines agrocinopines, which are sugar-phosphodiesters (e.g., CPD-15774, which is a phosphodiester of sucrose and L-arabinose). While opines are found in marine invertebrates, fungi, higher plants and mammals, they are most commonly found in plant crown gall tumors or hairy root tumors, where they are produced by bacteria of the genera TAX-357 and TAX-379, respectively. However, the first opine to be identified (in 1927), octopine, was isolated from an octopus muscle, hence its name, which was later extended for naming the opine family. Biosynthesis of opines in crown gall tumors is performed by enzymes encoded by genes contained in T-DNA (transfer DNA), which is part of the Ti plasmid (in TAX-357) or Ri plasmid (in TAX-379). The plasmids are inserted by the bacteria into the plant genome, where they are integrated and expressed. Those opines are used by the bacteria as energy, carbon and nitrogen sources. Each strain of TAX-357 and TAX-379 induces and catabolizes a specific set of opines, determined by the specific Ti or Ri plasmid they possess, and Ti-plasmids are classified as nopaline-type, octopine-type, agropine-type, vitopine-type, succinamopine-type
