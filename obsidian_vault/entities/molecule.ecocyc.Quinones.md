## Enriched Summary

Quinones are organic compounds derived from aromatic compounds, with two carbonyl groups incorporated into the ring structure with rearrangement of the double bonds where necessary. Quinones are conjugated but not aromatic. A classic example of a quinone is P-BENZOQUINONE. Quinones are commonly named with a prefix that indicates the parent aromatic hydrocarbon (for example, "benzo-" for benzene, "naphtho-" for naphthalene, and "anthra-" for anthracene) with a "-quinone" suffix. Several types of quinones serve as electron acceptors in electron transport chains. Those quinones are usually prenylated with a lipid-soluble isoprenyl tail that anchors them in the membrane lipid bilayer. Quinones can carry two electrons, and when fully reduced they are referred to as Reduced-Quinones quinols. The addition of a single H atom to a quinone results in formation of a free radical called Semiquinones. Many quinone derivatives often have other biological activities, such as CPD-11501 "vitamin K".
