## Enriched Summary

Arabinogalactans (AGs) are widely spread throughout the plant kingdom. Many edible and inedible plants are rich sources of these polysaccharides. AGs are associated with the pectin cell-wall component by physical bonds, and some of them are covalently linked to the complex pectin molecule as neutral side chains . AGs have been classified into two general forms, named Plant-Arabinogalactans-I "type I" and Plant-Arabinogalactans-II "type II". AGs of type I have a linear β-(1→4)-D-Galp backbone, while those of type II have a β-(1→3)-D-Galp backbone. In type II AGs, known also as arabino-3,6-galactan, the (β-1→3)-D-Galp backbone is heavily substituted at position 6 by mono- and oligosaccharide side chains composed of arabinosyl and galactosyl units. ## Annotation

Arabinogalactans (AGs) are widely spread throughout the plant kingdom. Many edible and inedible plants are rich sources of these polysaccharides. AGs are associated with the pectin cell-wall component by physical bonds, and some of them are covalently linked to the complex pectin molecule as neutral side chains . AGs have been classified into two general forms, named Plant-Arabinogalactans-I "type I" and Plant-Arabinogalactans-II "type II". AGs of type I have a linear β-(1→4)-D-Galp backbone, while those of type II have a β-(1→3)-D-Galp backbone. In type II AGs, known also as arabino-3,6-galactan, the (β-1→3)-D-Galp backbone is heavily substituted at position 6 by mono- and oligosaccharide side chains composed of arabinosyl and galactosyl units. It is more widespread than the AG type I and occurs in cell walls of dicots and cereals often linked to proteins (known as arabinogalactan proteins). AG type II, along with galactomannans and cellulose, comprises the predominating polysaccharides of green arabica coffee beans . In some case type II AG contains a small amount of β-glucuronate. There are many variations of AG type II produced by different plants. Two forms that have been studied thoroughly are Gum-Arabic "gum arabic AG", which is produced by acacia trees, and Larch-AG "larch AG, produced by larch trees (members of the genus TAX-3325). AG is very abundant in the wood of larch trees - certain parts of the western larch (TAX-3327) contain up to 35% AG . Larch AG type II is located in the lumen of the tracheids and ray cells, and is thus not a cell-wall component and by definition, not part of the Hemicelluloses of these trees.
