## Annotation

Penicillins are medically important Beta-Lactams "β-lactam" antibiotics biosynthesized as end products by certain filamentous fungi, including TAX-5076 and TAX-162425. Β-Lactam antibiotics inhibit the formation of peptidoglycan cross-links in the bacterial cell wall by the irreversible binding of their four-membered β-lactam ring to the so-called Penicillin-Binding-Proteins "penicillin binding proteins" (PBPs). The PBPs are proteins that contain a D,D-peptidase domain, most commonly used for hydrolyzing the D-ALA-D-ALA bond found in the pentapetide moiety of peptidoglycans. Since the β-lactam moiety of PENICILLIN "penicillins" is similar to the D-Ala-D-Ala structure, these enzymes bind penicillin irreversibly and become inactive. Several groups of enzymes belong to this family - carboxypeptidases, transpeptidases, and endopeptidases. The carboxypeptidase remove the terminal D-ALANINE from the pentapeptide, the transpeptidase form crosslinks between the pentapeptides of adjacent glycan threads, and the endopeptidases hydrolyze these crosslinks. This irrevesible binding prevents the enzyme from catalyzing the formation of these cross-links, resulting in a defective cell wall and cell death. Penicillin antibiotics were the first drugs that were effective against many previously serious diseases, such as syphilis, and infections caused by staphylococci and streptococci. They are still widely used today, although more and more types of bacteria have acquired resistance.
