## Enriched Summary

The Lower-case-i-Antigens "i antigen" is found on membrane proteins in red blood cells and many other cell types as well as various body secretions of developing fetuses and newborn infants , while the Upper-case-I-antigens "I antigen" is found in all adults . The i antigen undergoes gradual conversion to the I antigen in newborn infants, a process that completes in about 18 months. Both antigens are based entirely on Type 2 (BETA-D-GALACTOSYL-ETCETERA-GLUCOSAMINE "N-acetyllactosamine") chains. The basic i structure is an unbranched polylactosamine comprising at least two N - acetyllactosamine units connected by a β(1→3) linkage . The crucial step in the transition from the i antigen to the I antigen is catalyzed by EC-2.4.1.150, also known as the I-branching enzyme, which introduces the branching by adding a GlcNAc residue by a β(1→6) linkage to the galactose residue to which other residues are already attached by a 1,3 linkage . Note that the structure shown here is the core structure that defines I antigenicity. Most I antigens are further glycosylated. (Reviewed in ).
