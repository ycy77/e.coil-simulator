## Annotation

Extracts from bark of the willow tree (TAX-3688) and from TAX-23224 shrubs had been known to help alleviate headaches, pains, and fevers since antiquity. The natural active ingredient in these extracts is the plant hormone CPD-110. Unfortunately, while effective, salicylate has an undesired side effects, as it is known to upset the stomach. In 1897, chemists working at Bayer AG produced a synthetically altered version of CPD-1142, derived from the species TAX-57917 (meadowsweet), which caused less digestive upset than pure CPD-110. The new drug, acetylsalicylate, was named Aspirin, which is derived from "acetyl" and "Spirsaure", an old German name for salicylate derived from the genus name TAX-23224. CPD-524 Aspirin is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) used for pain relief, fever reduction, and against swelling. Like other NSAID drugs (e.g. CPD-7669 and Ibuprofen), aspirin works by inhibiting the enzyme EC-1.14.99.1 (also known as cyclooxygenase or COX) that converts ARACHIDONIC_ACID to PROSTAGLANDIN-H2, a precursor to other prostaglandins that mediate pain, inflammation, and fever. In addition, PROSTAGLANDIN-H2 is also a precursor of ALPHA11-ALPHA-EPOXY-15-HYDROXYTHROMBA, which stimulates platelet aggregation. The inhibition of the COX1 variant of the COX cyclooxygenase also reduces the formation of blood clots. Aspirin acts by acetylating a serine residue in the active site of the COX cyclooxygenase. Aspirin is much more active on the HS01815 (COX1) variant than on the HS01115 (COX2) variant. The popularity of aspirin grew over the first half of the 20th century, largely due to its effectiveness during the Spanish flu pandemic of 1918, but declined after CPD-7669 was released in 1956, followed by Ibuprofen in 1969.
