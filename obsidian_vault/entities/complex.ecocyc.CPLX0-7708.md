## Annotation

The "Cu efflux regulator," CueR, regulates genes related to the primary copper homeostasis system , which responds to the presence of copper, silver, or gold ions . The Cue system is predominantly active under aerobic conditions . Oxidative stress and Fe excess, provoked by Zn(II) stress, cause a dysregulation of CueR . The CueR system is involved in response to relatively low concentrations of CuSO4, while the CusRS system is inducted at higher concentrations . The CueR system senses Cu(I) in the cytoplasm . The crystal structures of CueR dimers bound to Cu+, Ag+, or Au+ have been solved . Each monomer consists of three domains, an N-terminal DNA-binding domain carrying two helix-turn-helix motifs, a central dimerization domain, and a C-terminal metal-binding domain. The DNA-binding and dimerization domains are characteristic of the MerR family of proteins. The dimerization domain contains a 10-turn α-helix that forms an antiparallel coiled-coil with the helix of the other monomer. The metal-binding domain contains an unusual metal receptor site in which the bound metal is inaccessible to the solvent. The metal ion is bound with a linear coordination to two conserved cysteine residues. This dithiolate coordination confers the metal ion selectivity for +1 ions . The sensitivity of CueR for copper(I) ions is extremely high (in the zeptomolar range), which corresponds to less than one atom per cell. Apparently it is the task of CueR and CopA to clear the toxic copper ions rapidly from the cytoplasm into the periplasmic space .
