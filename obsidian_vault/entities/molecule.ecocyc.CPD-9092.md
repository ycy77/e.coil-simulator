Chlorosomes contain about 1,500 bacteriochlorophyll molecules per reaction center. ## Annotation

Green sulfur bacteria (TAX-191412) and green flimentous bacteria (TAX-1106) contain chlorosomes - large organelles that are believed to be the most efficient light-harvesting antennae in nature . Green sulfur bacteria can grow at light intensities that are too low to support growth of any other phototroph, up to a million times lower light than normal sunlight . Chlorosomes contain about 1,500 bacteriochlorophyll molecules per reaction center. They are filled with 10-30 rod-shaped elements which are oriented parallel to the long axis of the chlorosomes and have a diameter of 10 nm. Each rod consists of a tubular bilayer formed by self-organization of the antenna bacteriochlorophyll molecules. Unlike all other known photosynthetic antenna, in which the chlorophyll molecules are bound to proteins, chlorosomes do not contain accessory proteins. The chlorosomal bacteriochlorophyll pigments are able to self-aggregate due to their unique chemical characteristics. The bacteriochlorophylls found in chlorosomes are either CPD-9092 "bacteriochlorophyll c", CPD-9093 "bacteriochlorophyll d" or CPD-9094 "bacteriochlorophyll e", depending on the species. They all possess a hydroxyl group at the C31 position that allows them to chelate the Mg atom of a neighboring molecule and to interact with the C13-oxo carbonyl group of another neighboring pigment molecule, affording the self-aggregation . In addition, all of these bacteriochlorophylls lack C-132-methylcarboxyl side chains, which are found in all other (bacterio)chlorophylls but would interfere with the ability to form aggregates .
