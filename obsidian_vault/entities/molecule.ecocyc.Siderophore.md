## Annotation

Although iron is extremely abundant on Earth, its solubility is very low at physiological pH in aerobic environments. While iron(II) is souble, it is rapidly oxidized in the presence of oxygen into insoluble ferric oxyhydroxides. In addition, iron is often complexed with organic molecules. The concentration of free iron in body fluids is estimated to be about 10-18 M . The most common iron uptake mechanism used by microorganisms are ferrisiderophore acquisition systems. Siderophores are small organic compounds with a very high affinity for iron. Once secreted into the environment, siderophores efficiently solubilize and chelate iron(III). The iron-siderophore complexes (ferrisiderophores) are imported back into the cell by specific transport systems, crossing both the outer and inner membranes . Once in the cytoplasm (and in some cases, in the periplasm), iron is released from siderophores by hydrolysis, proton-assisted dissociation of the complex, or reduction of the metal center . Siderophores are very common in pathogenic bacteria, since iron in the host organism is usually tightly bound to proteins, resulting in an extremely low concentration of free iron in the blood. More than 500 different siderophores have now been identified. The most common siderophores use hydroxamate and/or catechol functions, attached to either linear or cyclic scaffolds, to form a hexadentate structure. Siderophores with lower denticity (tetradentate, tridentate, and bidentate) are also produced, but have a much lower affinity toward iron(III). In general, all siderophores possess a higher affinity for iron(III) than for iron(II) .
