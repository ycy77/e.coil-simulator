## Enriched Summary

The DNA replication restart primosome, also simply referred to as the "primosome", is a complex that is involved in restart of stalled replication forks, as well as in replication initiation in certain phages and plasmids. There are multiple ways that DNA replication forks can stall and multiple ways that they get restarted. See the individual subunit entries for additional information on the function of the primosome. When a stalled DNA replication fork has no gaps in the leading strand, the primosome undergoes ordered assembly beginning with EG10763-MONOMER binding to DNA. Following this, CPLX0-3 binds to PriA, then EG10244-MONOMER binds. After this, CPLX0-13255 loads CPLX0-3621 (DnaB) in an ATP-dependent manner . For stalled DNA replication forks with small (> 6 base) gaps between the leading strand and the fork junction, a different DNA replication restart pathway exist in which EG10765-MONOMER binds DNA, typically when CPLX0-8165 are present. This binding of PriC enables PriA to bind to the fork, followed by DnaC bringing in DnaB complex . Once the DNA replication restart primosome is complete, EG10239-MONOMER associates with the complex and synthesizes an RNA primer .
