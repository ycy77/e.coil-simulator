## Enriched Summary

Gangliosides "Gangliosides" are molecules composed of Glycosphingolipids (Ceramides and Oligosaccharides) with one or more sialic acids (usually N-ACETYLNEURAMINATE) linked to the sugar chain. They are components of the cell plasma membrane that modulates cell signal transduction events, and appear to concentrate in lipid rafts. The name ganglioside is derived from the ganglion cells of the brain, from which the first gangliosides were isolated by the German scientist Ernst Klenk in 1942 . Indeed, gangliosides dominate the composition of the glycocalyces of most of the cells of the vertebrate nervous system. Although gangliosides with various glycan structures are found in every vertebrate tissue, they are markedly more abundant in the nervous system, where each cell has on average about 10 million cell surface gangliosides . Variations in the sialoglycan and lipid components of the gangliosides result in hundreds of distinct ganglioside structures with a variety of functions that ranges from regulating receptor tyrosine kinases to directing cell-cell communication, cell-substrate interaction, adhesion, and protein targeting .
